## Overview

Main-idea and primary-purpose questions ask what the passage is *doing as a whole*. They reward
readers who track the passage's structure instead of memorizing details. Get the main idea right
and the rest of the passage's questions become far easier.

## Core concepts

**Read for structure, not facts.** On the first pass, don't try to absorb every detail. Ask of
each paragraph: *what job does this do?* Is it stating a view, raising an objection, giving an
example, or drawing a conclusion? The details are there to look up later.

**Find the pivot.** Most GMAT passages turn on a contrast word — *however, but, yet, although, in
contrast*. The author's real point usually lives on the far side of that pivot. When you see
"Some argue X… **however**…", the main idea is rarely X.

**Main idea vs. primary purpose.**

- **Main idea** = the central claim, in one sentence ("*what* is the author saying?").
- **Primary purpose** = the author's goal, phrased as a verb ("*why* did the author write this?" —
  to *argue*, to *explain*, to *compare*, to *evaluate*).

**A correct answer covers the whole passage.** It reflects every major part — including any
closing qualification or debate — without overstating the author's position.

## Worked example

*Passage sketch:* Paragraph 1 presents a popular belief; paragraph 2 says the evidence is weak and
gives reasons; paragraph 3 concedes a small point but reaffirms the doubt.

- A good primary-purpose answer: "*question a widely held belief and explain why the evidence for
  it is weak.*" It matches all three paragraphs.
- A wrong (too strong) answer: "*prove that the belief is entirely false*" — the passage conceded
  a point, so "entirely false" overshoots.

## Common traps

- **Too narrow.** An answer that captures only one paragraph or a single detail. The main idea must span the passage.
- **Too broad.** An answer larger than the passage's actual scope ("the history of all X").
- **Too strong.** Extreme wording ("proves," "never," "the only") when the author was measured.
- **Right topic, wrong action.** The subject is correct but the verb is off (says "criticizes" when the author merely "describes").

## Key takeaways

- Read for the role of each paragraph, not the details.
- Locate the pivot word — the author's point usually follows it.
- The best answer covers the whole passage and matches the author's exact strength and purpose.
