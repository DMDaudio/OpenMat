## Overview

Nearly every rate problem rests on one relationship. Learn it, learn to organize the numbers,
and learn the one trap that catches most test-takers: average speed.

## Core concepts

**The master equation.**

\[\text{distance} = \text{rate} \times \text{time}\]

Rearranged as needed: \(\text{time} = \dfrac{\text{distance}}{\text{rate}}\) and
\(\text{rate} = \dfrac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}\).

**Organize with a table.** For multi-part trips, make a row for each leg with columns
Distance / Rate / Time. Fill in what you know, use \(d = rt\) to get the rest.

**Average speed is total-over-total.**

\[\text{average speed} = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}\]

This is almost never the simple average of the speeds, because time spent at each speed differs.

## Worked examples

**Basic.** A train travels 300 miles at 50 mph. Time \(= \tfrac{300}{50} = 6\) hours.

**Round trip (the classic trap).** You drive 120 miles at 60 mph, then return the same 120 miles
at 40 mph. Times are \(2\) hours and \(3\) hours; total distance \(240\) miles, total time \(5\)
hours:

\[\text{average speed} = \frac{240}{5} = 48 \text{ mph}\]

Not 50. You spend more time at the slower speed, so the average is pulled below the midpoint.

## Common traps

- **Averaging the speeds.** For equal distances at two speeds, the average speed is the *harmonic*
  mean, always **less** than the arithmetic mean. Compute total distance ÷ total time instead.
- **Mismatched units.** Convert minutes to hours (or vice versa) before dividing; mixing them is a silent error.
- **Combined/closing speed.** Two objects moving toward each other close the gap at the *sum* of
  their speeds; moving apart, the same; one chasing the other, the *difference*.

## Key takeaways

- Everything comes from \(d = rt\); rearrange it for what you need.
- Use a Distance/Rate/Time table for multi-leg trips.
- Average speed = total distance / total time — never just the average of the speeds.
