## Overview

Percents and ratios are the most frequently tested arithmetic ideas on GMAT Focus Quant, and
they hide inside word problems everywhere. Master three moves: converting between forms, handling
percent *change*, and scaling ratios.

## Core concepts

**Percent as a factor.** A percent is just a number over 100. The fastest way to apply a percent
change is to turn it into a **multiplier**:

- Increase by \(r\%\): multiply by \(1 + \tfrac{r}{100}\). (+25% → \(\times 1.25\))
- Decrease by \(r\%\): multiply by \(1 - \tfrac{r}{100}\). (−20% → \(\times 0.80\))

**Successive changes multiply.** Two changes in a row are multiplied, never added:

\[(+25\%)\text{ then }(-20\%): \quad 1.25 \times 0.80 = 1.00 \quad (\text{no net change})\]

**Percent change formula.**

\[\text{percent change} = \frac{\text{new} - \text{old}}{\text{old}} \times 100\%\]

Always divide by the **original** value, not the new one.

**Ratios scale together.** A ratio \(a : b\) means the quantities are \(ak\) and \(bk\) for some
multiplier \(k\). If boys : girls \(= 3 : 5\) and there are 24 boys, then \(k = 8\), so there are
\(5 \times 8 = 40\) girls.

## Worked examples

**Percent change.** A stock rises from $80 to $100. Percent increase \(= \tfrac{100 - 80}{80} = \tfrac{20}{80} = 25\%\).
Note it later falls from $100 back to $80: that's \(\tfrac{-20}{100} = -20\%\) — a *smaller* percent, because the base is now larger.

**Ratio scaling.** A recipe uses flour : sugar \(= 7 : 2\). To use 21 cups of flour, \(k = 3\), so
you need \(2 \times 3 = 6\) cups of sugar.

## Common traps

- **Adding successive percents.** +25% then −20% is *not* +5%; it's \(1.25 \times 0.80 = 1.00\).
- **Wrong base.** Percent change always divides by the original amount. A rise then an equal-percent fall does not return to the start.
- **Ratio ≠ actual count.** \(3 : 5\) does not mean 3 and 5 — it means \(3k\) and \(5k\). Find \(k\) first.

## Key takeaways

- Convert percent changes to multipliers and multiply them for successive changes.
- Percent change = (new − old) / old.
- A ratio \(a : b\) represents \(ak\) and \(bk\); solve for the multiplier \(k\), then scale.
