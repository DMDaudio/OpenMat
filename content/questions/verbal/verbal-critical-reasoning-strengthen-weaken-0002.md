## Question

SunFit installed standing desks throughout its office. Over the following six months, the
number of employees reporting back pain fell by 30% compared with the previous six months. This
shows that standing desks reduce back pain.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously **weakens** the argument?

## Explanation

The argument claims the desks *caused* the drop in back pain. To weaken a causal claim, the
strongest move is to supply an **alternative explanation** for the same drop.

**(B)** does exactly this: if employees who had chronic back pain left and were replaced by
younger workers, the reported back pain could have fallen simply because the *group of people*
changed — not because standing desks helped anyone. This directly undercuts the causal link.
**Correct.**

Why the others fail:

- **(A)** Cost is irrelevant to whether the desks *reduce back pain*.
- **(C)** Preference for the desks doesn't bear on their effect on back pain (and if anything
  faintly supports the argument).
- **(D)** This *strengthens* the argument — a comparison group without the desks saw no change,
  which is what you'd expect if the desks caused SunFit's improvement.
- **(E)** Background about back pain's importance; it says nothing about the cause of the drop.

## Hints

- The conclusion is causal ("the desks reduced pain"). The best weakener usually offers a *different* cause for the same result.
- Watch for answers that actually strengthen the argument, like a control group that saw no change.
