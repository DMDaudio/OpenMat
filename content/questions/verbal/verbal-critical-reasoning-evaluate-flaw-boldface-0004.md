## Question

The city's new anti-theft policy must be working. In the year since it took effect, the number
of thefts **reported to police** fell by 15%.

## Explanation

The conclusion ("the policy is working") assumes that fewer *reported* thefts means fewer
thefts *actually happening*. But reported thefts and actual thefts are not the same thing —
reporting rates can change on their own. If residents simply reported fewer thefts (say, because
they lost confidence that reports lead anywhere), the reported number could fall even if the
true number of thefts held steady or rose.

**(A)** names exactly this gap between *reported* and *actual* thefts. **Correct.**

Why the others are weaker:

- **(B)** Whether theft is worse than other crimes is irrelevant to whether this policy worked.
- **(C)** The argument never claims the policy affects only theft.
- **(D)** A single year is a fair concern, but it's a minor quibble next to the reported-vs-actual gap, which can invalidate the evidence entirely.
- **(E)** A definitional nitpick that doesn't undermine the core inference.

## Hints

- Pin down the conclusion's hidden assumption: it treats the *measure* (reported thefts) as identical to the *thing* it cares about (actual thefts).
- The best flaw answer attacks that assumption directly, not a side issue like sample period or definitions.
