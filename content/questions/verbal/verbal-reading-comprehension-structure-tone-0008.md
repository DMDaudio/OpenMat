## Question

*Read the passage and answer the question that follows.*

> Proponents of "brain-training" apps claim that a few minutes of daily puzzles can raise general
> intelligence. The evidence, however, is thin. Studies typically show that players get better at
> the specific games they practice, but this improvement rarely transfers to unrelated tasks —
> the supposed point of the exercise. This is not to say the apps are worthless: many people find
> them enjoyable, and enjoyment has its own value. But anyone hoping to sharpen their reasoning
> would be wise to treat the grander promises with caution.

## Explanation

Track the author's signals. "The evidence, however, is thin" and "treat the grander promises with
caution" show clear skepticism about the *big* claim (raising general intelligence). But "This is
not to say the apps are worthless… enjoyment has its own value" deliberately stops short of
condemning them.

That combination — doubting the broad claims while allowing some value — is **(A)**. **Correct.**

Why the others fail:

- **(B)** The author calls the evidence thin; not enthusiastic.
- **(C)** "Completely dismissive" overshoots — the author explicitly grants the apps some value.
- **(D)** Not neutral; the author clearly leans skeptical and gives a recommendation ("be cautious").
- **(E)** Nothing in the passage mentions risk or alarm.

## Hints

- Attitude questions hinge on the author's own qualifying words — here "however," "thin," "not to say… worthless," "caution."
- Watch for answers that are *too extreme* in either direction; the author's view is measured, not total.
