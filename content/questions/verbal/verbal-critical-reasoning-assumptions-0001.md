## Question

TransitCo argues that converting one of Main Street's three car lanes into a dedicated bus lane
will reduce the average commuting time for **everyone** who travels on Main Street, because
buses in the new lane will move faster than they do today.

The argument above depends on assuming which of the following?

## Explanation

The conclusion is about the **average** commute time for **everyone** on Main Street — not just
bus riders. But the plan takes a lane away from cars, so drivers are squeezed into fewer lanes
and may lose time. For the average across all travelers to fall, the time bus riders save must
not be cancelled out (or worse) by the time drivers lose.

**(B)** states exactly this. Use the **negation test**: negate (B) — "the time saved by bus
riders *is* outweighed by the extra time lost by drivers." If that's true, the average commute
time would rise or stay flat, and the conclusion collapses. Because negating (B) destroys the
argument, (B) is a necessary assumption. **Correct.**

Why the others fail:

- **(A)** Whether buses are currently slower than cars isn't required; the claim is only that
  buses will be faster *than they are now*. Negating it doesn't break the argument.
- **(C)** "A majority ride the bus" is far stronger than the argument needs. The average could
  improve even if a large minority rides the bus. A necessary assumption must be something the
  argument *can't do without* — this isn't.
- **(D)** Enforcement details don't need to be perfect for the conclusion to hold; this is not
  required.
- **(E)** The conclusion is scoped to people traveling *on Main Street*, so effects on other
  streets are out of scope.

## Hints

- The conclusion says "everyone," but the plan only obviously helps one group. What has to be
  true about the group it might *hurt*?
- Try the negation test on each choice: if negating it leaves the argument standing, it isn't a
  necessary assumption.
