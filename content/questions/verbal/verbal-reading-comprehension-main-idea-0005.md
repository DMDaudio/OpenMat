## Question

*Read the passage and answer the question that follows.*

> For much of the twentieth century, planners treated traffic congestion as a simple engineering
> problem: build more road capacity, and jams would ease. Yet cities that widened their highways
> often found that congestion returned to its former levels within a few years. The now widely
> accepted explanation is "induced demand": adding capacity lowers the effective cost of
> driving, which encourages more trips, longer commutes, and development farther from the
> center, until the new lanes fill up. Some planners therefore argue that pricing road use,
> rather than expanding it, is the only durable way to manage congestion. Critics respond that
> road pricing can fall hardest on lower-income drivers — a concern that has slowed its adoption
> despite its theoretical appeal.

## Explanation

Track the passage's arc. It opens with an old, standard view (build more roads), shows why that
view fails (congestion returns), introduces the concept that explains the failure (induced
demand), and closes with a resulting proposal (road pricing) *and* the objection to it. That is
an explanation of a complicating phenomenon plus the debate it has set off.

**(A)** captures both moves — undermining a standard approach and noting the ensuing debate.
**Correct.**

Why the others fail:

- **(B)** Too strong and one-sided. The passage reports the pricing argument *and* its critics; it does not endorse pricing as "the only acceptable" solution.
- **(C)** Engineering methods are never described — widening is only mentioned to show it fails.
- **(D)** No cross-city comparison of severity is made.
- **(E)** Far too broad; the passage is about one specific idea, not two centuries of economic thought.

## Hints

- Ask what the passage *does* paragraph by paragraph, not just what it's about.
- The correct primary-purpose answer must cover the whole passage — including the closing debate — without overstating the author's position.
