## Question

*Read the passage and answer the question that follows.*

> Monarch butterflies migrate thousands of miles to the same overwintering sites, yet no
> individual makes the round trip twice: the monarchs that return in spring are several
> generations removed from those that departed. This rules out learning the route from
> experience. Instead, monarchs rely on a "time-compensated sun compass." Cells in their
> antennae track the time of day and adjust the insect's reading of the sun's position
> accordingly, so it can hold a constant southward bearing even as the sun moves across the
> sky. When researchers coated the antennae to block light from reaching them, the butterflies
> could still detect the sun but could no longer keep a steady heading.

## Explanation

The last sentence is the key: with the antennae blocked, the butterflies **could still detect
the sun** but **could no longer hold a steady heading**. So the antennae's specific job is
time-compensation — adjusting for the sun's movement so a constant bearing can be maintained.

**(A)** states exactly that. **Correct.**

Why the others fail:

- **(B)** The passage explicitly says that with antennae blocked they could *still* sense the sun, so light-detection is not what the antennae provide.
- **(C)** The passage rules out learning the route (the returning generation never made the trip before).
- **(D)** The migration spans *several* generations, not one — and this isn't a function of the antennae.
- **(E)** Total distance is never linked to the antennae.

## Hints

- Anchor on the experiment: what did the butterflies *lose*, and what could they *still* do, when the antennae were blocked?
- The answer is the specific capability that disappeared — not the one that remained.
